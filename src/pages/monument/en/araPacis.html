Ara Pacis

Ara Pacis, where history meets the present and peace becomes a timeless massage.

History and construction

The Ara Pacis, in Italian "Altar of Peace," is an ancient Roman monument dedicated to Pax, the Roman goddess of peace. It was built between 13 and 9 B.C., during the reign of Augustus. The Ara Pacis was not only an altar but also a political symbol. Augustus used it to strengthen his image as a bringer of peace and stability, contrasting his rule with the chaos of the earlier civil wars. The altar was part of a broader propaganda campaign, which also included the Forum of Augustus and the Mausoleum of Augustus, all designed to celebrate his reign and its achievements.

How it is used

The Ara Pacis is an important symbol of Roman culture and history that continues to play a central role today. Now housed in a modern museum designed by Richard Meier and opened in 2006, the monument can be admired up close thanks to the building’s glass structure. It is studied by historians and archaeologists to gain deeper insights into the age of Augustus and to better understand its artistic and political significance. The museum also hosts temporary exhibitions, educational programs, and cultural events that link ancient Roman heritage to contemporary themes. As a popular tourist destination, the Ara Pacis attracts thousands of visitors each year and remains an enduring symbol of peace and prosperity.

Recent facts

Over time, the Ara Pacis has been restored many times to preserve its original details. In the 1930s, Mussolini ordered its reconstruction as part of his propaganda efforts. In recent years, the monument has been featured in films, documentaries, and advertisements. For example, it appeared in the film The Great Beauty (2013). Additionally, light projections have been used to recreate its original colors, allowing visitors to see how it looked in ancient Rome.

Legends and fun facts

  • It is said that the monument was originally decorated with bright colors, but they have faded over time.
  • Some scholars believe that the reliefs were inspired by Greek art.
  • In the 1930s, there was a proposal to move the entire monument to a different location, but it was decided to keep it in Rome.
  • The museum is considered controversial because of its modern design, which contrasts with the surrounding historic buildings.
